Enterprise Architecture - Navigation

Exploring an intent-based redesign of the Enterprise Architect left navigation. Before we group anything, we have to be honest about the fact that EAs juggle many distinct entity types — and they don't always want to triage them the same way.

The EA works in two distinct modes

When you watch an EA's day, the interactions don't blur together — they split cleanly into reactive triage and deliberate, focused work. Each mode has different cognitive demands and deserves different UI treatment.

  • Behavior: bulk operations, cross-entity context, modeling
  • Best UI: entity-scoped deep workspace with filters, tools, planning canvases

Why we need to design for both

The temptation is to pick one and force it everywhere. That fails. Here's why the IA has to honor both modes.

01

Different cognitive demands, different UI patterns

Triage rewards speed and breadth — scan, decide, move on. Focused work rewards depth and continuity — load context, sustain a thread, manipulate many items. A single UI optimized for either degrades the other.

02

Forcing entity silos breaks the triage flow

If "what needs me right now?" requires the EA to walk through six entity-specific menus, they'll miss things. Cross-entity triage demands a unified queue at the top of nav.

03

Forcing a single inbox breaks deep work

A monthly tech debt review needs filters, bulk actions, lifecycle context, and modeling tools that are specific to tech debt. Trying to do it in a generic queue is friction. Each entity earns its own dedicated workspace.

04

Mature products already do this

Salesforce Lightning pairs a "Today" inbox with deep object workspaces. Jira pairs "Your work" with project boards. ServiceNow's own Agent Workspace pairs a unified queue with record-type spaces. The pattern is well-validated.

Why pure intent-based fails

The pure intent-based strawman in the comparison above looks clean on paper, but it falls apart in contact with how EAs actually work.

01

Cross-entity workflows split across multiple intents

Application rationalization needs Plan (the strategy), Monitor (health signals to trigger it), and Govern (ARB approvals to retire). Three navigation destinations for one continuous workflow. The EA navigates back and forth instead of working in one place.

02

Each entity repeats across every intent

"Application portfolio health" lives in Monitor, "Application rationalization" in Plan, "App-related ARB requests" in Govern. The L2 ends up being the entity grid restated three times — same nouns, different verbs in front.

03

Pure intents increase mental load

When an EA opens the workspace thinking "I need to work the application portfolio," pure intent-based forces them to translate that into "the app stuff is split across Plan, Monitor, and Govern — which one do I open first?" Translation cost compounds across every session.

How an EA sees the architecture

Most Enterprise Architects are trained on TOGAF, the dominant enterprise architecture framework. It organizes the world into four stacked domains — Business, Data, Application, Technology — and EAs reason, navigate, and group everything they touch through that layering. Any IA we design has to respect this lens, or it forces EAs to translate from their mental model on every interaction.

01
Business Strategy, governance, organization, and key business processes.
02
Data Logical and physical data assets and data management processes.
03
Application Individual applications and their relationships with the business processes they support.
04
Technology Hardware and software infrastructure that supports business, data, and applications.
Why this lens matters for the IA

The proposed hybrid uses Business, Application, and Technology portfolios at L1 because they map directly to TOGAF domains. Data is treated cross-cutting in EAW (data certification records flow through Govern). It's also why Business capabilities and Product capabilities sit in different portfolios despite sharing the word "capability" — they live in different architecture layers, are owned by different roles, and have different lifecycles.

The honest hybrid

The synthesis. Four bands at L1: a unified triage queue for global reactive work, entity portfolios and governance for focused sessions, workbench shortcuts to embedded tools, and personalized favorites the user pins. This isn't "pure" anything — it's a structure that respects how EAs actually work, organized around TOGAF's architecture domains, with intent-based grouping reserved for work that genuinely cuts across entities.

What changes between roles

Scope: The EA sees the full estate; the Application Owner sees only their slice — every list, every metric, every queue is pre-filtered to their applications.
Depth over breadth: The App Owner gets deeper sub-navigation inside Applications and Technical debt, because that's where they spend their entire time.
No strategy layer: Business capabilities, TRM standards, shadow IT discovery, and modeling tools are hidden — they belong to the EA's remit, not the App Owner's.
